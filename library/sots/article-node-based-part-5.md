# Node-Based Scenario Design – Part 5: Plot vs. Node

Justin Alexander explores the fundamental tension between plotted scenarios (where events follow a predetermined sequence) and node-based scenarios (where individual interesting locations can be visited in any order).

## The Plotted Approach Problem

Alexander illustrates how a plotted Las Vegas CTU scenario might force a specific sequence: self-storage facility → Mansoor's apartment → confrontation with corrupt cop Nasser → climactic Bellagio bomb defusal. While this creates narrative momentum, it removes player agency by demanding they follow the designer's predetermined path.

## The Node-Based Alternative

Rather than enforcing this sequence, node-based design creates independently interesting locations that function effectively regardless of visitation order. In the same scenario, if players capture Mansoor at the Bellagio early, the discovery of additional bombs creates new dramatic stakes—turning what seemed like victory into a larger crisis.

## Meaningful Player Choice

Alexander argues that node-based scenarios offer genuine meaningful choices through:

- **Tactical decisions**: Whether to search the Bellagio carefully or rush creates different outcomes and consequences
- **Timeline pressure**: Adding a 6:00 pm attack deadline forces resource allocation choices (split parties, call police, prioritize leads)
- **Campaign continuity**: Early choices create ripple effects (escaped Mansoor returns later, corrupt cop remains a threat)

## The Engagement Factor

Alexander emphasizes that meaningful choice isn't merely about "visiting all three nodes eventually." Rather, **allowing players to determine the sequence and approach makes the experience more memorable and fun** because players drive outcomes, not designers.
