# The Three Clue Rule

**Source:** https://thealexandrian.net/wordpress/1118/roleplaying-games/three-clue-rule
**Author:** Justin Alexander

## Core Principle

"For any conclusion you want the PCs to make, include at least three clues."

The reasoning: Players will likely miss the first clue, overlook the second, and misinterpret the third before reaching the intended conclusion. Three clues provide one primary plan plus two backup plans.

## Why Three Works

- Removes multiple chokepoints from your design
- Allows players to reach correct conclusions through various paths
- Lets players confirm suspicions with multiple evidence sources
- Maintains adventure momentum even with partial clue discovery

## Extended Principles

### Chokepoint Solutions
For any critical problem blocking adventure progress, provide at least three solutions.

### Permissive Clue-Finding
Embrace player creativity. If players develop clever investigative approaches, reward them with relevant information. Treat prepared clues as a safety net, not a straitjacket.

### Proactive Clues
When players become stuck, introduce active events that generate new clues organically — the villain advancing their plans, an ally providing unexpected information.

### Red Herrings: Overrated
Players naturally generate their own false theories without your help. Reserve red herrings for extreme caution only.

## Application Example: Werewolf Mystery

Three main conclusions, three clues each = nine distinct clues across three investigative paths:

1. **The killer is a werewolf** — wolf track transitions, oversized claw marks, silver bullets
2. **Victims were former lovers** — love letters, diary entries, photographs
3. **Investigation leads to butcher shop** — broken meat crates, meeting notes, calendar entries

## Key Corollaries

- **Revelation Lists:** Write out what you want players to discover before play begins
- **Player Ingenuity:** Let clever solutions work even if unplanned
- **Flexibility:** Remain open to unexpected investigative approaches
