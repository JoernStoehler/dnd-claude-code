# Don't Prep Plots: Prepping Scenario Timelines

*Article by Justin Alexander | March 23rd, 2009*

## Core Concept

Rather than preparing rigid plot structures, game masters should develop detailed scenario timelines showing how events unfold when player characters take no action. This approach enables dynamic, responsive storytelling.

## Key Principles

### Scenario-Based Preparation

The author maintains a "Campaign Status document" tracking daily events, upcoming activities, NPC movements, plot threads, and hotspot developments. This becomes the foundation for timeline construction.

### Timeline Structure

Timelines detail specific dates and times showing what happens sequentially. They document how antagonists pursue their goals independently of player intervention. As the author notes, "If the PCs do absolutely nothing, these are the events that happen."

### Responsive Revision

When players disrupt anticipated events, the GM revises the timeline accordingly. The author created a "second timeline" after his players raided the temple complex, completely altering the cultists' positions and resources.

## Practical Implementation

### Tracking Changes

The author uses strikethrough formatting for past events while maintaining visibility of future activities. This prevents losing reference information while clarifying current focus areas.

### Appropriate Detail Levels

Early timeline entries require minimal specificity since players likely won't interact with them. Later entries—after significant disruption—need comprehensive detail because defensive states remain uncertain until the next session.

### Natural Stopping Points

Timelines extend only until situations stabilize into new status quos, typically several in-game days after major events. This prevents excessive preparation for scenarios players might never reach.

## Campaign Integration

One player character, Tee, watched the compound during specific hours, necessitating precise timeline details about what she'd observe. This demonstrates how player actions directly influence preparation requirements rather than forcing predetermined outcomes.
