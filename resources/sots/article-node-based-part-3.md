# Node-Based Scenario Design – Part 3: Inverting the Three Clue Rule

## The Three Clue Rule Recap

The Three Clue Rule states: "For any conclusion you want the PCs to make, include at least three clues." This provides sufficient redundancy so that even if players miss or misinterpret some clues, they can still reach the intended conclusion.

## The Inversion

Alexander inverts the rule: "If the PCs have access to ANY three clues, they will reach at least ONE conclusion."

Instead of designing three clues that all point to a single destination, design multiple destinations (nodes) with clues distributed among them. With three clues available, the players will reliably reach at least one of those destinations — even if it's not the one the designer expected first.

## How It Works: A Four-Node Example

**Starting Node (Blue):** Contains three clues — one pointing to Node A, one to Node B, one to Node C. Per the Three Clue Rule, the players will reach at least one of these nodes.

**After visiting Node A:** Node A contains two additional clues (one pointing to B, one to C). The players now have access to five total clues. Four of those point to unvisited nodes B and C. The inverse rule ensures they will reach at least one more node.

**After visiting Node C:** Node C contains clues pointing to A and B. The players now have access to seven total clues. Four point to already-visited nodes (redundant), but three point to Node B — satisfying the original Three Clue Rule for reaching the final unvisited node.

The structure is self-reinforcing: each visited node adds clues that point to remaining nodes, making it progressively harder for the scenario to stall.

## Comparison to the Plotted Approach

Both the plotted approach (from Part 2) and the node-based approach require the same amount of preparation: four nodes and nine clues. The difference lies entirely in structure. The plotted approach arranges these into a linear sequence with branching contingencies. The node-based approach connects them as a web.

For the same preparation effort, node-based design produces a richer, more flexible scenario and leaves the PCs in the driver's seat. It delivers more mileage out of the same amount of work.
